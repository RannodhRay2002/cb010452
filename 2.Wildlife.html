Yala National Park

Yala is a national park and a stringent nature reserve. The park is divided into five blocks and covers over 130,000 hectares of land, with light woods, scrubs, grasslands, tanks, and lagoons. Currently, two blocks are open to the public. Yala was named a wildlife sanctuary in 1900 and a national park in 1938. It is located in Sri Lanka's south-east, bordering the magnificent Indian Ocean.

Ironically, under British rule, the park was first used as a hunting ground for the upper crust. Yala is home to 44 different mammal species and 215 different bird species. The world's largest concentration of leopards, stately elephants, sloth bears, sambars, jackals, spotted dear, peacocks, and crocodiles are among its more notable residents. The greatest time to visit Yala is between February and July, when the park's water levels are low, allowing animals to roam freely.

Udawalawe National park

Udawalawe National Park, Sri Lanka's sixth largest animal sanctuary, is located 180 kilometers south of Colombo in the island's south-central region. The park is located immediately south of the Central Highlands, whose escarpment creates a beautiful backdrop. The Udawalawe Reservoir is located at the park's heart. Udawalawe National Park, which was established in 1972 with the goal of protecting the catchment area of the Udawalawe reservoir, which provides water for agriculture and hydro-power generation, covers an area of 30,821 acres and resembles an African game park, with thorny-shrub jungle and grasslands.

Udawalawe is without a doubt the best spot in Sri Lanka to watch wild Asian Elephants all year: the park has about 500 elephants, who often travel in herds of up to 100. Udawalawe National Park is remarkable in that the number of elephants roaming the park is constant throughout the year; there is no seasonal variation in elephant herds. Mornings and evenings are the finest times to visit the park.

Late evening also affords the photographic opportunities in the backdrop of loveliest sunsets. Keeping company to the herds of elephants, the main attraction of the park are the Water Buffalo, Wildboar, Spotted Deer, Sambur Deer, Jackal, Samber, Black-naped hare, mongooses, bandicoots, foxes, s the endemic Toque Macaque and Gray Langers. Sighting a Leopard and other smaller cats like Fishing cat & Jungle cat would be a bonus.

Minneriya National park

Set in the heart of the popular cultural triangle of Sri Lanka, Minneriya National Park is mostly known for its incredible elephant migration, which is one of Asia’s finest wildlife experiences. During the drier months of June to September. Covering an area of 8,890 hectares, Minneriya is around 4 hours from the capital city of Colombo and is close to other local attractions.

Small herds congregate around the water tank, forming a massive elephant mass. It's heartwarming to watch the lively jumbos drink, bathe, jostle, and hurry. It'll be a fantastic recollection within a diversified agenda of contrasting highlights. You might see toque macaque monkeys, spotted deer, sambar deer, wild buffalo, porcupine, wild boar, and mongoose, among the other 24 species that call the park home. Leopards can also be found in the park, though in less numbers than in Yala.
